## Part 1 — What happened

### 1. US strikes Iran for a third consecutive night; naval blockade of Iranian ports takes effect

US Central Command struck military targets across Iran — including Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa and Bandar Abbas — targeting coastal surveillance, drone and missile capabilities, hours before Washington's reinstated naval blockade of Iranian ports took effect ([CNN](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/07/13/world/live-news/iran-war-trump), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/13/nx-s1-5891746/us-iran-strait-of-hormuz-updates)). President Trump also said he will "probably soon" order a strike on the Pickaxe Mountain nuclear site ([FDD overnight brief](https://www.fdd.org/overnight-brief/july-13-2026/)). This is the deepest sustained US strike campaign since the ceasefire memorandum collapsed on July 8. **Confidence: High** (corroborated by CNN, NPR, Bloomberg, Al Jazeera; strike-damage specifics are US military claims — Medium).

### 2. Trump announces a 20% toll on all Hormuz cargo; transit traffic collapses

Trump declared the US the "guardian" of the Strait of Hormuz and said the US will charge fees "at the rate of 20% on all cargo shipped" through the strait ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/13/trump-iran-hormuz-strait-charge-reimburse.html), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/trump-says-us-will-be-reimbursed-20-rate-for-hormuz-traffic), [Korea Herald](https://www.koreaherald.com/article/10807775)). The IMO formally opposed the fees ("IMO stands firmly against charging fees for passage through straits used for international navigation") and tanker executives called the plan unworkable ([CNBC/IMO](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/13/imo-maritime-organization-strait-hormuz-tolls-trump-iran.html)). Kpler data showed only 14 ships crossed the strait on Sunday, versus 37 a week earlier. **Confidence: High** on the announcement and IMO response; **Medium** on the traffic data (single provider).

### 3. Iran strikes tankers in Omani waters and US bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan and Oman

The IRGC said it "struck and disabled" two supertankers (al-Bahiya and Mombasa) that had switched off transponders in the strait; the UAE defense ministry said an Indian crew member aboard the Mombasa was killed ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/13/missile-strikes-and-attacks-on-ships-reported-around-strait-of-hormuz), [CNN](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/07/13/world/live-news/iran-war-trump)). Overnight, Iran launched drones and missiles at a US Patriot battery, fuel and ammunition depots in Kuwait, the al-Juffair base in Bahrain, Prince Hassan Air Base in Jordan, and radar systems in Oman ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/13/new-iran-strikes-on-gulf-as-us-attacks-escalate-what-we-know), [US News](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2026-07-13/iran-escalates-attacks-on-us-bases-in-gulf-states-warns-of-more-incidents-in-strait)). Tehran warned it would treat any regional support for US forces as an "act of war." **Confidence: High** that attacks occurred (multi-outlet, host-government confirmations); **Medium** on damage claims (largely IRGC assertions); **Medium** on the tanker fatality (single-government attribution so far).

### 4. Oil extends gains: Brent near $85 as supply-risk premium rebuilds

Brent September futures rose 1.85% to $84.84 and WTI rose 2% to $79.78 on the toll announcement and blockade reinstatement ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/14/oil-prices-today-brent-wti-hormuz-trump-toll-iran.html), [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/7/13/oil-prices-jump-as-us-and-iran-trade-attacks-over-strait-of-hormuz), [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/brent-crude-oil)). The move is notable for being driven by *policy* risk (toll/blockade) on top of kinetic risk, meaning the premium is unlikely to unwind on any single quiet night. **Confidence: High** (exchange data).

### 5. EU launches ~$1bn "Team Gaza Initiative" as UN and Hamas trade accusations over aid

The European Commission and over a dozen countries pledged €883.6m (~$1bn) for Gaza recovery — water, sanitation, debris removal, health systems — against an estimated $71bn ten-year reconstruction need ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/13/eu-unveils-1bn-fund-for-gaza-war-recovery), [US News](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2026-07-13/eu-launches-1-billion-aid-initiative-for-gaza)). Simultaneously, UN Deputy Special Coordinator Ramiz Alakbarov said armed personnel affiliated with the de facto authorities forced a halt to food distribution in Jabalia; Hamas denied interference ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/13/hamas-denies-un-accusations-over-gaza-aid-distribution-interference), [UN](https://press.un.org/en/2026/sc16390.doc.htm)). **Confidence: High** on the pledge; **Medium** on the interference dispute (contested claims).

## Part 2 — Deep dive: incentives and motives

### 1. The US strike campaign and blockade

**Washington's drivers.** The administration is converting military dominance into coercive leverage before Iran's new leadership consolidates. Striking coastal surveillance and anti-ship capability is instrumentally tied to the blockade: it degrades Iran's ability to threaten enforcement vessels. The threat against Pickaxe Mountain signals the nuclear file is being folded into the escalation ladder — raising the price of Iranian holdout. Domestically, Trump benefits from framing the US as Hormuz's "guardian" collecting payment for a global public good, turning a costly deployment into a revenue narrative.

**Tehran's drivers.** With Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei still unseen in public since March — reportedly recovering from severe injuries ([Time](https://time.com/article/2026/07/05/khamenei-s-funeral-is-meant-to-project-strength-but-iran-s-new-leader-has-yet-to-appear/)) — the IRGC is the de facto decision-maker and needs to demonstrate deterrence to domestic and regional audiences during a fragile succession. Its constraint is severe: it cannot win a direct exchange, so it optimizes for *cost imposition* (tankers, bases, insurance markets) rather than military effect. Attacking ships that "turned off transponders" is a legal-gray-zone framing designed to keep Oman and other neutrals from fully tipping to the US side.

### 2. The 20% Hormuz toll

This is the most economically consequential move of the day. A toll monetizes chokepoint control and — if even partially enforced — functions as a tax on Gulf exporters and Asian importers, collected by neither. The incentive design is likely coercive rather than fiscal: it pressures Gulf states (who lose export competitiveness) and Asian buyers (who pay more) to lean on Tehran and on Washington for a settlement. The constraint is legitimacy and practicability: UNCLOS transit-passage rights, IMO opposition, and the sheer mechanics of assessing "20% of cargo value" make full implementation doubtful — the market impact runs through *uncertainty and insurance pricing*, not actual collection. Shipowners' immediate rational response is avoidance and delay, which is why transits collapsed to 14/day before any fee is collected.

### 3. Iran's retaliation against Gulf hosts

Iran's targeting of US facilities *in* Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan and Oman — while framing host governments as victims of US presence rather than enemies — is a wedge strategy: raise the domestic political cost to Gulf monarchies of hosting US forces, without triggering Article-51-style Gulf belligerence. Gulf states' incentives cut the other way: they condemned Tehran (as Kuwait did last week) but have avoided joining offensive operations, because their sovereign-wealth-funded diversification programs (NEOM, Vision 2030, Qatari LNG expansion) are existentially exposed to a long war. Expect continued Gulf hedging: public condemnation of Iran, private pressure on Washington to define an off-ramp.

### 4. Oil markets

The premium is being rebuilt on *structural* rather than episodic risk: a blockade plus a toll regime changes the expected cost of every future barrel, not just today's. OPEC+ spare capacity (Saudi/UAE) is partially stranded because it also transits Hormuz; only Saudi's East–West pipeline and UAE's Fujairah route bypass the strait, and both are already heavily subscribed. That asymmetry means price responds more to Hormuz signaling than to production announcements — a regime economists should model as a chokepoint-risk market, not a supply-demand market.

### 5. EU Gaza fund

The EU is buying relevance in a post-war order currently dominated by the US-chaired transitional Board of Peace. €883.6m is small against $71bn, but it is *first-mover institutional* money: whoever funds water, health and debris removal shapes procurement standards and the reconstruction pipeline. The UN–Hamas aid dispute matters because reconstruction finance at scale is conditional on credible non-interference in distribution; each verified interference incident raises the risk premium on donor commitments and delays Gulf money, which is waiting for governance clarity.

## Part 3 — Policy implications for South Korea

Korea imports roughly 70% of its crude and 36% of its LNG from the Middle East ([Seoul Economic Daily](https://en.sedaily.com/opinion/2026/07/13/hormuz-reblockade-china-helium-curbs-demand-faster-supply)), making the re-blockade and toll the dominant macro shock. Current exposure snapshot:

- **Markets (July 14):** KOSPI opened at 6,769 (-0.56%), rebounded, then fell back to the 6,700 level intraday amid a chip selloff; the won is trading near ₩1,499/USD ([Businesskorea](https://www.businesskorea.co.kr/news/articleView.html?idxno=272978), [Asia Business Daily](https://www.asiae.co.kr/en/article/2026071410162291548)). Refining and shipping stocks rallied on the price shock. **Confidence: Medium** (local outlets, intraday figures).
- **Energy security:** Seoul has secured ~273m barrels of Middle Eastern and Kazakh crude routed *around* Hormuz (~3 months of consumption), banned naphtha exports, released reserves (22.5m barrels via IEA coordination), passed a $17bn supplementary budget, and accelerated nuclear restarts ([OilPrice](https://oilprice.com/Latest-Energy-News/World-News/South-Korea-Locks-In-273-Million-Barrels-of-Crude-That-Wont-Touch-Hormuz.html), [CSIS](https://www.csis.org/analysis/impact-iran-conflict-south-korea-numbers), [Asharq Al-Awsat](https://english.aawsat.com/business/5262527-s-korea-secures-270-mn-barrels-oil-suppliers-unaffected-hormuz-blockade)). Strategic reserves reportedly cover ~26 days of actual consumption. **Confidence: Medium-High** (consistent across CSIS and regional press; reserve-day figures are estimates).

**Implications by development:**

1. **Blockade + strikes:** Direct upward pressure on Korea's import bill, freight and war-risk insurance; renewed danger to Korean-flagged/chartered vessels and to the ~1,000+ Korean nationals in Gulf states. The toll, if enforced, would hit Korean refiners and petrochemical margins directly — 20% on cargo value dwarfs any freight-rate shock — and Korea Herald's front-page treatment signals Seoul recognizes this.
2. **Iranian strikes on Kuwait/Bahrain/Jordan:** Kuwait and Bahrain host significant Korean construction and energy-services contracts; escalation there threatens receivables and project timelines, and any Iranian action against Qatar (65% of Korea's helium; major LNG supplier) would transmit straight into semiconductor production costs — compounding China's helium export halt.
3. **Gaza reconstruction fund:** A medium-term opportunity: Korean construction firms are well-positioned for water/desalination and debris-removal contracts if the EU pipeline opens to non-EU bidders. Low urgency, but worth tracking procurement rules as they are drafted.

**Testable indicators to monitor (falsifiable, checkable within days–weeks):**

1. **Hormuz daily transit count (Kpler/MarineTraffic).** If transits stay below ~20/day for two consecutive weeks, the toll/blockade regime is binding and Korea's Q3 import costs will rise materially; recovery above ~30/day would falsify the "sustained closure" scenario.
2. **Won/dollar at ₩1,500 and Bank of Korea response.** A sustained break above ₩1,500 plus emergency FX measures would confirm the crisis is transmitting into Korea's financial channel, not just the trade channel; stabilization below ₩1,480 without intervention falsifies it.
3. **Actual collection of the 20% toll on any vessel.** If within 14 days no mechanism (US Navy escort-fee, port-state enforcement) has actually charged a ship, treat the toll as coercive signaling and discount its direct price impact; a first documented collection would force repricing of all Korea-bound Gulf cargo.
4. **Qatari LNG loadings at Ras Laffan.** Any week-over-week drop >15% in Qatari LNG exports (Kpler/ICIS data) would signal the conflict is reaching Korea's gas supply; steady loadings falsify near-term LNG risk even as crude risk persists.
