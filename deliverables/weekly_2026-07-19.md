## 1. The Week's Arc

What structurally changed this week is the answer to the question "what is this war about." On Monday it was still about the strait: the US had reimposed its naval blockade and Trump had announced a 20% transit toll, and the KOSPI's −9.95% circuit breaker day priced a chokepoint crisis. By Wednesday the toll was gone — withdrawn within 24 hours, replaced by vague Gulf "trade and investment deals" — and the episode fit a clean coercive theater read. Thursday broke that read: Trump issued an infrastructure ultimatum ("next week comes the power plants... the bridges"), Reuters revealed Iran had ordered the Houthis to close Bab el-Mandeb if the US strikes Iran's grid, and the Bank of Korea hiked for the first time since January 2023, explicitly citing Middle East oil in its inflation diagnosis. Friday the ultimatum executed five days early — six bridges in Hormozgan, a Bandar Abbas rail junction, the Chabahar port tower — and Iran answered across seven countries, setting Kuwait's Shuaiba power and desalination complex on fire: the first Iranian strike on Gulf civilian infrastructure. By Saturday the war had found both its new target class and its first American blood: a US strike destroyed the Bonji desalination plant serving 20 Iranian villages, Iran hit Kuwait's water and oil infrastructure a second straight day, and Iranian ballistic missiles killed two US service members at Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan — the first US combat deaths from direct Iranian fire since March. Iran formally suspended the June MOU; Mojtaba Khamenei surfaced only as a written statement promising "unforgettable lessons"; Trump answered with "1,000 missiles locked and loaded" and an eighth night of strikes that put manned fighters 650 km inside Iran.

Three structural shifts to carry forward. First, the Gulf states crossed from bystanders to targets: Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain and Jordan all took fire this week, and their utilities — water above all — are now hostages in an escalation ladder they do not control. Second, the two chokepoints became formally coupled: the standing Houthi order makes Bab el-Mandeb a function of what the US does to Iran's grid, so Korea's main workaround (the Red Sea detour) is no longer independent of its main exposure (Hormuz). Third, the diplomatic architecture died in form as well as fact: the MOU suspension means de-escalation now requires building something new, not returning to something signed.

Korea's week ran on a different axis. The BOK's hawkish turn — hike, explicit tightening bias, "until inflation returns to 2%" — gave the won a policy anchor that held through everything: ₩1,502.8 on Monday to ₩1,480.4 by the Thursday close, the first sub 1,490 weekly close since the crisis range began. Equities had no such anchor: a +6.24% relief rally Wednesday, a −6.37% chip rout Thursday, and a Constitution Day holiday Friday that left Monday July 20 to price three accumulated days of war news in one session. The FX equity decoupling is the week's cleanest Korean finding — and Monday is its hardest test yet.
